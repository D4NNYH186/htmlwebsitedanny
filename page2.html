1.1 Describing the purpose of IT in modern business.

There are various aspects of IT incorporated within business models in the present day and IT has become a vital part of every business plan regardless of how small or large the company is.
In this section I will discuss some of the different ways and reasons IT is implemented in the present day marketplace. [1]
One of the more widely used aspects of IT used across businesses is email and has been for a long time, this is used in a variety of ways, whether it be communication between employees, with customers, suppliers and even other associates linked with the company. As time has progressed, so have the options for IT communication, various tools have evolved and are now becoming a lot more advanced, VOIP (Voice over Internet protocol)/IP telephony Is one of these, this allows people to make telephone calls at any time providing there is a solid connection to the internet,
VOIP converts a users voice into a digital signal which is then sent to the other user via the internet. Another commonly used tool is live chat systems (Zendesk, Drift),
online meeting tools and video-conferencing systems (Nasdaq meeting portal, Zoom, lifeWire) are now a key part of the operation of a business also, being used to conference with teams and management not based in the same premises.

Moving away from communication tools we can now take a look at other aspects of IT and tools used in business looking at various management systems whether this be IMS (Inventory Management Systems) used to manage and order stock, also having these connected to a POS (point of sale) system, this ensures that when an item is sold, this is reflected in the stock levels and will no longer be counted in the inventory, DMS (Data Management Systems) that store data on servers and storage devices,
that along with the introduction of ‘the cloud’ that allows users to access their data anywhere on the planet, providing they have internet access, this alleviates the use for filing cabinets and paper filing systems, MIS (Management Information Systems) that can manage employee productivity, expenses, identify areas of improvement and profitability. CRM (Customer Relationship Management) this is a way for businesses to track and manage correspondence from customers, whether it be storing emails and scanned documents to creating call logs when customers have contacted via phone. [2]

Unit 1.4 Describe the risks that might impact digital projects – what causes digital projects to fail?

There are an array of different reasons and risks that could affect a digital project and its success. Researching this I have found that ‘70-90% of digital projects have failed to deliver what they have promised, have failed beyond minor improvements or completely flopped.CIO.com reported that 50% of companies aren’t successfully executing their digital strategies, and 20% believe that those very projects are a waste of time.’ Accounting for business procedures, or to be more specific, failing to account for these, this is where somebody spots a problem in how something is delivered from the company, they uncover a solution to this, they sell this idea to the team and make the improvements, but the solution, while it solves the problem, it does not match the businesses procedures and strategies, then staff end up maintaining a tool that is hindering business and staff morale, staff become disengaged, bringing the company and tool further from the original goal. Investing in gadgets and gizmos and not customers, an example of this would be a project manager or CEO, wants a CRM system implementing because they have seen that “these are the future”. Another risk is poor definition/ creating unattainable expectations, it is tempting to promise a client the world and create expectations that you cannot fulfil, leaving the project looking less and less attainable, the clients expectations need to be controlled, limit these to being clearly defined and try not to confuse the client. 1.7 Describe legal guidelines and constraints that impact digital projects – How does GDPR impact digital projects?

On May 25th 2019 the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) came into legal force and effect. The regulation will take over from the Data Protection Act (1995) and is set to have an impact on individuals and businesses in all sectors globally, the digital/tech sector being one of those. [1] The focus of GDPR is to protect all EU citizens from privacy and data breaches in a data-driven society. Controllers will gather and attain data and processors will use the data for purpose. This will lead to various data subject rights that could impact the project:

  • Data portability- this is the right for a data candidate to receive personal data regarding them, which they have previously provided and have the right to send this data to another controller.
  • Right to be forgotten- this is gives the candidate the right to ask for any data given to be erased and no longer used.
  • Breach notifications- if there has been a data breach the processors have to notify their client and controllers within 72 hours from the time they first realise the breach, non-compliance can lead to large fines and penalties.[2]
